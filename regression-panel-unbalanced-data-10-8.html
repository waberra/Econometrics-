Balanced vs Unbalanced Panels

Balanced: Ti = T ‚àÄi
Unbalanced: Ti varies across i
In a balanced panel, every unit i is observed for the same number of time periods T. In an unbalanced panel, some units have fewer observations due to missing data, entry, or exit.
Use: Almost all real-world panels are unbalanced; the textbook balanced case is mainly for theory.
Example: Some firms appear only after they enter the market; some workers drop out of the sample.

FE/RE with Unbalanced Panels

FE, RE, and pooled OLS
all extend naturally to unbalanced data
FE and RE estimators do not require a balanced panel. They simply use whatever observations are available for each unit.
Use: You can keep units with Ti ‚â• 2 in FE, and include units with any Ti in RE (depending on software implementation).
Example: A worker with only two time periods still contributes to FE estimation.

Unbalancedness itself is not a problem, but attrition can be. If units drop out for reasons correlated with the error term or regressors, estimators can become biased.
Use: Think carefully about why units enter/exit; ask whether attrition is plausibly exogenous.
Example: Workers with low wages are more likely to leave the sample ‚áí potential attrition bias.

Practical Considerations

More periods ‚áí more weight
in FE/RE estimation
In unbalanced panels, units with more periods contribute more information (more observations) to the estimation. This changes implicit weighting relative to a balanced design.
Use: Summarize the distribution of Ti and think about which units drive the results.
Tip: Report how many units have each Ti, and check whether high-T units are systematically different.
