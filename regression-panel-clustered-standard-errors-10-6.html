Why Cluster?

In panel data, errors are usually correlated within individuals (or firms, states, schools). Treating observations as independent underestimates standard errors and overstates significance.
Use: FE/RE panel regressions, difference-in-differences, repeated cross-sections by region.
Example: Wages for the same worker over time are hit by persistent shocks.

Cluster-Robust SEs

Allow arbitrary correlation within cluster,
assume independence across clusters
Clustered standard errors treat each cluster (e.g., worker, firm, state) as the unit of sampling. Within a cluster, residuals can be heteroskedastic and serially correlated.
Use: Many observations per cluster, many clusters overall (e.g., N_clust ‚â• 30).
Example: Cluster by worker ID in a worker panel; cluster by state in a state-level DID.

Robust vs Cluster-Robust

HC (Eicker‚ÄìWhite): robust to heteroskedasticity only
Cluster: robust to heteroskedasticity + within-cluster correlation
Ordinary heteroskedasticity-robust SEs still assume independent errors across all observations. Cluster-robust SEs relax this within each cluster.
Use: If you have panel data or grouped data, cluster at the group level, not just HC.
Example: In a DID with state-level treatment, cluster by state, not by individual.

Practical Discussion

Clustered SEs are often larger than naive ones, reducing t-statistics and statistical significance. This is a feature, not a bug: it corrects for over-optimistic inference.
Use: Always think: ‚ÄúWhat is my effective sampling unit?‚Äù and cluster at that level.
Rule of thumb: Cluster by the level at which the error term is correlated and treatment varies.
