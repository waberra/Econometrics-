üìò Key Ideas: Identification, Excluded Exogenous Variables, Order & Rank Conditions

Identification of a Structural Equation

Parameters are identified ‚áî unique values implied by population moments
An equation is identified if, in principle, its structural parameters can be recovered from the joint distribution of the observed variables. Without identification, no estimator can uncover the true structural effects.
Use: Decide whether IV/2SLS can recover a demand or supply equation, investment equation, etc.
Example: Demand is identified only if at least one exogenous variable affects supply but not demand.

Order Condition (Single Equation)

# Excluded exogenous ‚â• (# endogenous RHS ‚àí 1)
Count, for the equation of interest, how many exogenous variables in the system are excluded from that equation. Compare to the number of endogenous regressors (right-hand side). This gives a necessary condition for identification.
Use: Quick ‚Äúcounting‚Äù check before going deeper into rank conditions.
Example: Demand: 1 endogenous RHS (price), 1 excluded exogenous (cost) ‚áí just-identified by the order condition.

Rank Condition (Conceptual)

Relevant instrument matrix must have full column rank
Beyond counting, the excluded exogenous variables must actually move the endogenous regressors. Technically, a certain matrix involving reduced-form parameters must have sufficient rank.
Use: Economic reasoning and first-stage regressions to check instrument relevance.
Example: Cost shifters must significantly explain price in the first stage for demand identification.

Under-, Just-, and Over-Identified

Under: too few excluded exogenous
Just: exactly enough
Over: more than enough
Under-identified equations cannot be estimated structurally; just-identified have a unique IV solution; over-identified allow tests of overidentifying restrictions.
Use: Choose estimation method (2SLS, GMM) and whether to test instrument validity.
Example: Demand with multiple cost shifters is over-identified; you can test them using a J-test.
