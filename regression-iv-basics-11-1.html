Endogeneity Problem

OLS requires X to be exogenous: E(u | X) = 0. If X is correlated with u (omitted variables, simultaneity, measurement error), OLS is biased and inconsistent.
Use: Returns to education, price‚Äìquantity relationships, policy variables chosen in response to outcomes.
Example: Ability affects both education and wages ‚áí corr(educ, u) ‚â† 0.

What is an Instrument?

Z correlated with X (relevance)
Z uncorrelated with u (exogeneity)
An instrument Z helps isolate variation in X that is not contaminated by u. IV uses only the part of X explained by Z.
Use: Policy changes, distance variables, random assignment, lags of X (sometimes).
Example: Distance to college as an instrument for education.

Use: When you have at least one valid instrument for each endogenous regressor.
Example: Wage on education, instrumented with distance to college.

IV vs OLS

OLS: biased but precise
IV: consistent but less precise
IV often has larger standard errors than OLS, because it uses only part of the variation in X. Good instruments must be both relevant and credible.
Use: When exogeneity is doubtful and you can argue for a strong, plausibly exogenous instrument.
Tip: Weak instruments (small first-stage F) can make IV unreliable.
